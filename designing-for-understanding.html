Project Summary

Why this mattered

Older adults worried about privacy and security, but still agreed to terms they did not understand. The core issue was not lack of concern. It was bad information design.

Challenge
Transform static legal copy into a readable, trustworthy UX flow.
Approach
Survey + moderated tests + prototype comparison + stats.
Result
Prototype 3 emerged as the clear winner across readability, trust, and usability.
Problem

Users were afraid — but still uninformed

The original Google Pay T&C experience overwhelmed elderly users with long paragraphs, poor hierarchy, and little sense of control. Participants skipped, guessed, or depended on relatives instead of reading with confidence.

Goal

Shift T&C from legal obstacle to guided understanding

I reframed success around three UX outcomes: make the content scannable, make risk understandable, and help users feel confident enough to make a decision without external support.

Core design hypothesis: comprehension does not improve by reducing content alone. It improves when information is structured, summarized, and visually sequenced in a way that matches the user’s reading behavior.
Research

Mixed-methods process with measurable validation

This was designed as a full UX research loop: identify barriers, design alternatives, validate with users, and compare outcomes statistically rather than relying on subjective preference alone.

01

Literature + framing

Mapped the problem space around the digital divide, cognitive load, trust, and legal language. This exposed a key gap: older users were highly concerned about privacy but poorly served by current consent interfaces.

02

Survey baseline

Collected quantitative data from 32 elderly participants aged 50–80 to understand security concerns, tech confidence, reading behavior, and dependence on others.

03

Prototype design

Created 3 alternative T&C experiences in Figma, each representing a different content strategy: plain legal text, highlighted summaries, and a fully guided visual model.

04

Moderated testing

Ran individual sessions to capture readability, engagement, confidence, and task completion signals. Each participant reviewed all 3 prototypes.

05

Quant + qual analysis

Used descriptive analysis, comparative ratings, and significance testing to identify the most effective information architecture. Patterns were reinforced with participant quotes and observed hesitation points.

Design Iteration

Three prototypes, one clear winner

Instead of polishing one idea too early, I created three distinct concepts to test which information model actually helped users understand high-risk content.

Prototype 1 / Plain Legal Text

Lowest performance

Outcome

The redesign turned concern into comprehension

Prototype 3 outperformed the alternatives because it reduced reading burden, improved trust, and helped users understand risk before agreement. The biggest lesson: users do not need fewer rights statements. They need clearer pathways through them.

Key UX Insight

Structure beats simplification

The strongest design move was not reducing content. It was sequencing it. By grouping information into purpose-based chunks and making decisions explicit, the interface lowered fear and increased user autonomy.
