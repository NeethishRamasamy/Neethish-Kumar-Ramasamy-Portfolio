Research Background 📚

Why gamification in e-learning — and why is the forfeit method so under-researched?

🎮

Gamification Works

A comprehensive literature review identified that gamification elements significantly improve student motivation and engagement in e-learning — but research is skewed toward reward-only systems.

❓

The Research Gap

The forfeit method — penalising users for incorrect answers — remains relatively unexplored in existing literature. This study directly addresses that gap in an e-learning context.

🎯

The Question

Does negative marking drive deeper learning through consequence-awareness — or does positive reinforcement create better, less stressful outcomes?

📱 The App — Mathematics E-Learning (Purple UI)

📐
Algebra

Ch.1: Pythagorean Theorem — visualising a²+b²=c². YouTube lesson → 5-question quiz → colour-coded feedback → leaderboard or badge.

📏
Geometry

Right triangles, triangle sides. Multiple-choice: absolute triangle / right triangle / none. Marks awarded on selection with immediate colour feedback.

📊
Statistics

Data interpretation and probability. Progressive difficulty building on prior Algebra and Geometry knowledge acquired earlier in the experiment.

Methodology 🧪

A controlled experiment — two groups, same curriculum, different gamification mechanics. Three data collection methods triangulate the findings.

📋 Data Collection

📱

App Usage Data

Track user time spent, topic completion rates, and interaction patterns — objective behavioural metrics across both groups.

📝

Post-Testing Survey

Gather feedback on user experience, engagement, and perceived effectiveness of each gamification method from participants.

🎙️

Semi-Structured Interviews

Deeper insights into individual user preferences and emotional reactions to both gamification approaches through qualitative probing.

👶 Participant Selection — Why Elementary School Children?

The study focused on elementary school children because this age group is at a critical stage for forming foundational learning habits. This choice aligns with the research goals and considers the children's development stage — an age where gamification influence is strongest and where the anxiety effects of competitive systems (forfeit) versus positive reinforcement (badges) are most pronounced and measurable.

Results 📊

Both methods produced measurable outcomes — but with important trade-offs. Neither method wins outright.

Forfeit ⚔️ Non-Forfeit

🏆 Forfeit Method — Findings

  • 📈

    Enhanced Engagement

    Users exhibited increased focus and effort on comprehending concepts to avoid negative consequences. Deeper cognitive processing indicated a stronger grasp of subject matter.

  • 🧠

    Improved Knowledge Retention

    Data analysis suggested enhanced knowledge retention potentially attributable to the emphasis on avoiding mistakes, leading to more deliberate and lasting learning.

  • ⚠️

    Increased Stress & Anxiety

    Some users reported increased stress and anxiety associated with the fear of losing points — potentially impeding learning for anxiety-prone children.

🏅 Non-Forfeit Method — Findings

  • 🌱

    Stress-Free Environment

    This method fostered a stress-free learning environment, beneficial for users who thrive under positive reinforcement and learn more effectively in low-pressure situations.

  • 🤝

    Inclusive Learning (UDL)

    By eliminating penalties, this approach promoted an inclusive experience accessible to a wider range of learners — aligning with Universal Design for Learning (UDL) principles.

  • 📉

    Reduced Motivation (Some Users)

    Some users — particularly those motivated by consequences — exhibited reduced motivation to learn without competitive pressure.

Conclusion 🎓

Neither method reigns supreme — the best choice depends on context, learners, and goals.

📝 The Finding

While neither the forfeit nor non-forfeit method reigns supreme in e-learning, the most effective approach hinges on several key factors:

  • 1

    Desired Learning Outcomes

    If the goal is deep understanding and solidifying knowledge, the forfeit method is the stronger choice. For a relaxed, inclusive environment, non-forfeit is better.

  • 2

    Target Audience

    Consider the learners' age, preferred learning styles, and individual preferences. The forfeit method's pressure may be less effective for younger students or anxiety-prone children.

  • 3

    Educational Context

    The subject matter and overall goals should influence the selection. The forfeit method's knowledge retention benefits must be weighed against its suitability for the specific topic and audience.

🪞 Reflections

  • 💡

    Context is everything

    The same mechanic that motivates one learner stresses another. Gamification design must be participant-specific — one-size-fits-all approaches consistently underserve some users.

  • 💡

    Age matters enormously

    Elementary school children are particularly susceptible to the anxiety effects of competitive systems. Future research should test whether findings hold across secondary and adult learners.

  • 💡

    Hybrid approach shows promise

    The badge + forfeit combination in Group 2 suggests a hybrid — consequences without social comparison — may offer the best of both worlds. Worth exploring as a third experimental condition.

"While neither the forfeit nor non-forfeit method reigns supreme in e-learning, the most effective approach hinges on the desired learning outcomes, the target audience, and the educational context."

— Gamification Research, Conclusion, 2024
