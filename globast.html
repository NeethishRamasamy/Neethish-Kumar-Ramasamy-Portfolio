Globast

An accessibility-first streaming platform designed for visually and hearing-impaired users. Through iterative testing and inclusive interaction design, the final concept reduced task completion time by 35% and significantly improved perceived independence while browsing content.

Case Snapshot

Accessibility was the product, not an add-on

Instead of patching mainstream streaming patterns, Globast started from disability-specific needs: voice guidance for visual impairment, subtitle language depth for hearing impairment, and reduced navigation friction for both.

🎙️ Audio descriptions designed as primary UX support
💬 Multilingual subtitles and clearer caption states
🔎 Simplified discovery, stronger browsing hierarchy
🧭 Navigation built around independence, not assistance
Challenge 1

Mainstream streaming is visually biased

Visually impaired users struggle with discovery, navigation, and context. Audio description exists in some products, but often as a buried setting instead of a central access path.

Challenge 2

Subtitles are not the same as accessibility

Hearing-impaired users need more than generic captions. They need language control, clarity, readability, and reduced cognitive friction while switching content.

Challenge 3

Users wanted independence

The real product goal was not “inclusive features.” It was enabling users to browse, choose, and watch on their own — without needing sighted or hearing support from others.

Research

20 participants, mixed accessibility contexts

The research combined interviews, task-based usability testing, and prototype validation with users across visual and auditory accessibility needs. Two personas were synthesized to anchor decisions without losing nuance.

01

02

03

Prototype refinement

Adjusted hierarchy, controls, and content cards based on observed hesitation and errors.

Personas

Two different needs, one coherent system

Charles

Visual impairment. Needed strong voice support, simplified navigation, and content confidence before playback.

Ming Yue

Hearing impairment. Needed reliable subtitle language access, clarity, and control without extra friction.

Design principle: Globast was shaped around “independent completion.” Every UX decision was evaluated against whether the user could complete the journey alone.

Impact

Accessibility improved when the product was designed around independence

The final Globast concept proved that accessibility support should not behave like an extra settings layer. When content discovery, playback, and adaptation controls are integrated into the core journey, confidence and completion both improve.
