Project Overview

This case study explores the effectiveness of two gamification methods in an e-learning context: the forfeit method (punishing incorrect answers) and the non-forfeit method (no penalty for wrong answers). The study aimed to compare their impact on user motivation, retention, and engagement in elementary school students.

Problem

Traditional e-learning methods can be dry and lead to disengagement. Gamification, incorporating game-like elements, is a promising approach to address this by increasing user motivation and engagement. However, the optimal method for achieving this remains unclear.

Solution

A study compared two e-learning app designs (forfeit vs non-forfeit) and found neither is universally better. The best approach depends on what you're trying to teach, who you're teaching, and the learning environment. Forfeit methods may be better for deep learning, while non-forfeit methods may create a more relaxed and inclusive environment.There's a trade-off and the best choice depends on your specific goals and learners.

Research Process

Literature Review

A comprehensive literature review was conducted to identify existing research on the impact of gamification elements on student motivation and engagement in e-learning applications. This review revealed that the forfeit method, which involves penalizing users for incorrect answers, remains relatively unexplored within the existing body of research. This case study aims to address this gap in knowledge by investigating the effectiveness of the forfeit method in an e-learning context.

Methdology

  • Application Development: An interactive e-learning application was created, divided into three topics: Algebra, Geometry, and Statistics.

  • Participant Selection: The study focused on elementary school children because it aimed at understanding how this specific age group learns best. This choice aligns with the research goals and considers the children's development stage.

  • Gamification Implementation: The application employed a controlled experiment design, with two groups experiencing different gamification implementations

User Testing

Data Collection



  • Application usage data: Track user time spent, topic completion, and interaction patterns.
  • Post-testing survey: Gather feedback on user experience, engagement, and perceived effectiveness of the gamification methods.
  • Semi-structured interviews: Gain deeper insights into individual user preferences and reactions to the different approaches.

Results

Forefit Method

Findings

  • Enhanced engagement:Users exhibited increased focus and effort on comprehending concepts to avoid negative consequences associated with incorrect answers. This deeper cognitive processing was further supported by user feedback indicating a stronger grasp of the subject matter.

  • Improved knowledge retention: Data analysis suggested enhanced knowledge retention potentially attributable to the emphasis on avoiding mistakes, leading to more deliberate and lasting learning.

Limitations:

  • Increased stress and anxiety: Some users reported experiencing elevated stress and anxiety due to the potential for negative marking, potentially impacting their overall learning experience.

  • Discouragement of sensitive users: This method might discourage users with low tolerance for negative reinforcement, leading to disengagement from the learning platform or even complete app abandonment.

Non-Forefit Method

Findings

  • Stress-free environment: This method fostered a stress-free learning environment, potentially beneficial for users who thrive under positive reinforcement and learn more effectively in low-pressure situations.

  • Inclusive learning experience: By eliminating penalties for incorrect answers, this approach promoted an inclusive learning experience accessible to a wider range of learners with diverse learning styles and abilities, aligning with the principles of Universal Design for Learning (UDL).

Limitations:

  • Reduced motivation: Some users, particularly those accustomed to or motivated by the presence of consequences, might exhibit reduced motivation and engagement due to the absence of incentives for exerting greater effort on answering questions correctly.

  • Unsuitable for some learners: This method might not be as effective for learners who benefit from the increased focus and challenge imposed by the forfeit system, as it might not provide sufficient cognitive stimulation or motivation to learn for these individuals.

Project Overview

While neither the forfeit nor non-forfeit method reigns supreme in e-learning, the most effective approach hinges on several key factors. The desired learning outcomes should take center stage. If the goal is deep understanding and solidifying knowledge, the forfeit method might be the stronger choice. Conversely, fostering a relaxed learning environment might be better achieved with the non-forfeit method. Understanding the target audience is equally important. This includes considering the learners' age, preferred learning styles, and individual preferences. For example, the pressure associated with the forfeit method might be less effective for younger students or those who find it stressful. Finally, the educational context itself plays a role. The subject matter and overall learning goals should influence the selection. The forfeit method's potential benefits for knowledge retention need to be weighed against its suitability for the specific topic and the desired learning experience.
