Verdura

Project Summary

Turning botany from overlooked to unforgettable

Most botany education still depends on diagrams, textbooks, and static environments. Verdura reimagined the experience as a spatial learning journey where students could enter ecosystems, inspect plants, and test knowledge inside the same world.

Problem
Students struggle to notice, remember, and care about plant life.
Approach
Use VR immersion, guided narration, and interactive exploration to raise attention and recall.
Result
More memorable learning, stronger engagement, and better knowledge testing outcomes.
Problem

Botany had an engagement problem, not just a curriculum problem

Plant blindness describes the tendency to overlook plants in everyday life and undervalue their importance. In educational contexts, this translates into low interest, weak recall, and poor emotional connection to subject matter that feels abstract and distant.

Opportunity

Immersion could make plants impossible to ignore

VR offered a fundamentally different learning mode: one where students could move through ecosystems, inspect details in context, and connect explanation with experience. The product opportunity was to turn observation into participation.

Research

Building the experience around how people actually learn

The research combined educational framing, user testing, prototype comparison, and interaction validation. The goal was not just to make a visually impressive VR app, but to prove that immersion improved comprehension and recall.

01

Problem framing

Reviewed literature around plant blindness and science education to define where traditional learning breaks down: low salience, low emotional engagement, and low transfer into memory.

02

User group definition

Validated the concept with 28 participants across students and educators to represent both learning and teaching perspectives.

03

Lo-fi interaction testing

Rapid paper and journey prototypes mapped the core loop: enter an environment, inspect a plant, receive contextual explanation, then test understanding.

04

VR prototype iteration

Built and refined environment cards, plant interaction cues, narration flows, and quiz mechanics based on usability pain points.

05

Outcome validation

Measured recall, exploration time, and participant preference to understand whether immersion created a stronger learning effect than static content.

Impact

Verdura proved that immersive science learning can raise both attention and retention

The final product direction showed that VR is most powerful when it is not just visually impressive, but structurally educational. By merging exploration, explanation, and reinforcement into one seamless journey, Verdura made plant learning feel consequential and memorable.
